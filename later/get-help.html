In Immediate Danger?

If you or someone you know is in immediate danger or thinking about harming themselves:

  • Call 911 or your local emergency number
  • Go to the nearest emergency room
  • Call the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 1-800-273-8255

Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to common questions about seeking mental health support.

Consider seeking professional help if you experience persistent changes in mood, thoughts, or behaviors that interfere with your daily life, relationships, or work. Signs may include feeling overwhelmed, prolonged sadness, excessive worry, changes in sleep or appetite, withdrawal from activities you once enjoyed, or thoughts of harming yourself. Remember, seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness.

Therapists (including counselors and social workers) provide talk therapy to help with emotional and behavioral issues. Psychologists have doctoral degrees and can provide psychological testing and therapy. Psychiatrists are medical doctors who can diagnose mental health conditions, prescribe medication, and sometimes offer therapy. The right professional for you depends on your specific needs.

Therapy costs vary widely depending on location, provider type, and session length. Many insurance plans cover mental health services, though coverage details differ. Check with your insurance provider about coverage, copays, and in-network providers. If you don't have insurance or adequate coverage, many therapists offer sliding scale fees, and community mental health centers provide low-cost options.

The first session typically involves getting to know each other and gathering information. The therapist will ask about your concerns, history, and goals for therapy. You'll discuss confidentiality, session logistics, and the therapeutic approach. It's normal to feel nervous, and you don't have to share everything immediately. This session helps determine if the therapist is a good fit for your needs.

Support your loved one by listening without judgment, expressing concern and care, encouraging them to seek professional help, and learning about their condition. Avoid dismissing their feelings or offering unsolicited advice. Set boundaries to protect your own mental health, and remember that you can't force someone to get help. In crisis situations, contact emergency services or a crisis hotline.
